Chapter 15

Random Walk & Diffusion

Can deterministic macroscopic behavior — diffusion — emerge from pure randomness, and does the macroscopic law remember the microscopic step?

Interactive · live simulation

Thousands of walkers from the origin. Switch the step rule between lattice, Gaussian, and Pearson — the cloud spreads the same way and the measured ⟨r²⟩/4t lands on the same D. Turn on drift to see advection-diffusion.

Key equations

  • \big\langle\lvert\vec r - \langle\vec r\rangle\rvert^2\big\rangle = 4 D tmean-square displacement grows linearly (2D)
  • D = \frac{a^2}{4\tau}\;\text{(lattice)}, \qquad D = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\tau}\;\text{(gaussian)}diffusion coefficient from the step rule
  • \sigma_x(t) = \sqrt{2 D t}the Gaussian width from the central limit theorem

Three rules, one law

Lattice steps, Gaussian increments, and fixed-length Pearson jumps are very different microscopically. Yet with matched parameters they all reach the same diffusion coefficient and the same ⟨r²⟩ ∝ t growth. The microscopic rule sets only the number D; the form of the law is universal — the random-walk face of the central limit theorem.

Linear, not ballistic

The signature of diffusion is that the spread grows linearly in time: ⟨|r-⟨r⟩|²⟩ = 4Dt in 2D, with D = a²/4τ for fixed-length steps. Not the t² of a moving particle, not saturating — linear. The lab measures D = variance/4t live and lands it on the formula.

Brownian motion's skeleton

This is the bridge from the gas lab to real Brownian motion: a heavy particle jostled by a gas takes effectively random steps, with D = k_BT/γ by the Einstein relation. Strip away the forces, keep the randomness, and diffusion is what remains.
